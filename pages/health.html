Occupational Health for Woodworkers

🫁 Dust & Respiratory Health

Prolonged exposure to fine wood dust — especially from hardwoods — carries serious risks including asthma, sinusitis, and nasal cancers. Always wear a properly rated respirator (P2 or better) when sanding, routing, or machining. Get respiratory checks annually if you're in the trade.

👂 Hearing Protection

Power tools, routers, and planers can exceed 95dB — levels that cause permanent hearing loss within minutes of unprotected exposure. Use rated hearing protection every single time. Once it's gone, it's gone.

🦴 Musculoskeletal Health

Back injuries, shoulder strain, and repetitive stress injuries are endemic in woodworking. Invest in good workshop ergonomics — bench height, anti-fatigue matting, and taking proper breaks. See a physio early; don't push through pain.

🧴 Skin & Chemical Exposure

Finishes, resins, solvents, and timber extractives can cause skin sensitisation and chemical burns. Gloves, long sleeves, and proper ventilation aren't optional — they're standard practice.

💜 Women's Occupational Health in the Workshop

Respiratory protection, ergonomic tool sizing, and chemical exposure risks are often calibrated for male bodies. Women should be especially mindful to verify PPE sizing fits correctly, and to seek out research and advice specific to female physiology in trade environments.

🌸 Mental Health & Industry Isolation

Being a woman in a male-dominated industry carries unique pressures. If you're feeling isolated, the Women in Timber network and Beyond Blue both offer community and professional support to help navigate the craft and the workplace.
